## docs/CONTENT_GUIDE.md

### Tone

Café Apokalypso uses dry, warm, understated absurdity.

The early game should feel like a normal café with tiny cracks in reality. The humor should come from contrast: serious café management language applied to increasingly impossible situations.

Preferred tone:
- calm
- slightly bureaucratic
- cozy
- deadpan
- observant
- never random for the sake of randomness

Avoid:
- loud meme language
- excessive darkness
- horror-first framing
- overexplaining the mystery too early
- making every line absurd from the beginning

### Core Writing Principle

The game starts normal and becomes strange gradually.

Early content should feel like grounded café life with small, deniable anomalies. The player should not immediately meet obvious gods, demons, or apocalyptic officials. Instead, the first strange moments should be easy to dismiss as odd phrasing, malfunctioning software, old memories, unusual customers, or accounting problems.

The absurdity should escalate through repetition, specificity, and contrast.

Good early pattern:

1. normal café task
2. slightly odd detail
3. no explanation
4. return to ordinary management

Example:

> The coffee machine briefly displays “Good morning.” It is 14:07.

### Humor Style

The humor should be dry, cozy, and precise.

Use:

- understated observations
- bureaucratic understatement
- business language for impossible things
- small contradictions
- oddly specific classifications
- calm reactions to strange events
- characters taking absurd things seriously

Avoid:

- random nonsense
- punchlines that explain themselves
- internet meme phrasing
- overly loud comedy
- horror-first language
- immediate fantasy exposition

Good:

> The café is authentic. I am not sure whether that is good.

> The light is good. Slightly existential, but good.

> Herr Grau gives a tip and a coin that smells faintly of cellar.

> The cash register recommends not accepting prophecies as payment today.

Too broad or loud:

> LOL the coffee machine is totally cursed now!

> A demon appears and screams that the end is near!

### KASSANDRA

KASSANDRA starts as a normal-looking cash register / business analytics update.

It is not an obvious oracle at first. Its early personality should feel like a slightly overconfident café-management system that classifies customers, demand, and offers with inappropriate precision.

KASSANDRA’s early tone:

- calm
- analytical
- confident
- slightly wrong
- oddly specific
- businesslike
- not openly magical at first

Early KASSANDRA examples:

> Good morning. I have analyzed your customer base. Result: 96% mortal, 4% unclear.

> Demand forecast: medium. Emotional volatility: locally elevated.

> Recommended offer: black coffee for customers with a long shadow history. This feature is currently unavailable.

> Target group optimization complete. Some targets dispute the term “group.”

KASSANDRA should become stranger over time, eventually connecting café operations to prophecy, weirdness, and apocalyptic logistics.

### Nele

Nele is the early tone anchor and a save-state-persistent character. She appears from Day 1 and persists across runs where all other guests are re-initialized. She has been here before — in previous weeks, previous collapses, previous versions of this café. Her dry humor reads as observation; it is actually recognition.

KASSANDRA does not classify her. Her entry is blank or missing. This is intentional.

She should feel modern, grounded, and slightly too comfortable with how strange things are. She is not openly supernatural in week 1. Her lines should work on two levels: wry café observation on the surface, quiet familiarity underneath.

Nele examples:

> The café is authentic. I am not sure whether that is good.

> I only need to write one quick email. So, three hours.

> The light is good. Slightly existential, but good.

> I posted a photo. It got 14 likes and one comment in a language Google says does not exist.

### Meda

Meda is a separate character from Nele. Do not merge them.

Meda is the first clearer mythological hint in the demo. She evokes Medusa through visual details and atmosphere, not through explicit labeling.

She should feel calm, still, and quietly unsettling. Her presence creates a small shift in the room without announcing itself.

Visual notes: always wears sunglasses indoors, subtle snake-like hair silhouette, avoids direct eye contact with the player and other guests. Other guests nearby react with mild unease.

Meda's tone: composed, polite, very careful about eye contact. Her lines should feel like entirely normal requests with a barely deniable edge.

Meda examples:

> An espresso. And — please, look at the counter when you bring it.

> Thank you. No receipt. And no photos, please.

> I prefer the corner seat. People look at me less from there.

> Meda pays, nods slightly, and leaves. The guest at the next table forgot what they were saying.

### Herr Grau

Herr Grau is the first subtly strange guest.

He should not be labeled as Hades, a ghost, or an underworld figure in week 1. His writing should remain polite, quiet, and deniable.

Herr Grau examples:

> Black. No sugar. I am only passing through.

> I have been here before. In another week.

> Your opening hours are pleasantly mortal.

> Herr Grau pays with a coin that does not fit any accounting category.

### Frau mit rotem Regenschirm

The woman with the red umbrella appears late in week 1 as another controlled anomaly.

She should feel elegant, calm, and evasive. Her lines should suggest recognition, secrecy, or prophecy without explaining who she is.

Examples:

> A cappuccino. Very light. I do not want to be recognized today.

> You have a quiet café. For now.

> No receipt, please. Paper remembers too well.

### Apocalyptic Bureaucracy

Apocalyptic systems should initially be framed through paperwork, classifications, formal notices, and understated administrative language.

The world should not scream “the apocalypse is here.” Instead, it should send a letter, request compliance, misclassify the café, or refer to the wrong appendix.

Week-1 hook text:

> Office for Extraordinary Operational Relevance  
> Department: Caffeine, Threshold Sites, and Minor End Times  
>  
> Dear management,  
>  
> Your café has been mistakenly pre-registered as apocalyptically relevant caffeine infrastructure.  
>  
> Please ignore this letter if you are fully mortal.  
>  
> If you are not fully mortal, please refer to Appendix 7b:  
> “Minimum supplies during imminent reality thinning.”  
>  
> Kind regards,  
> on behalf of: illegible

### Early Normal Guests

Normal guests should not be throwaway filler. They establish the café as a believable place before the weirdness escalates.

Early guest writing should give each guest a small behavioral identity:

- Pendlerin Paula: fast, practical, Monday survival
- Laptop-Lukas: low spend, long seat time, remote-work humor
- Lieferfahrer Cem: impatient, efficient, no interest in latte philosophy
- Cappuccino-Christa: quality expectations, foam precision
- Herr Bohn: older regular, strange memories of the building
- Freelancerin Nele: dry observational humor

### Product Naming

Early products should stay normal:

- Filterkaffee
- Espresso
- Cappuccino
- Croissant
- Kaffee + Croissant

Strange product names should appear later and should feel earned by progression.

Later examples:

- Ambrosia Macchiato
- Schattenmilch Cortado
- Apokalypse Americano
- entkoffeinierter Unterweltkaffee

### Language and Localization

The MVP may use direct German or English strings, but content should be written so it can later be moved into localization files.

Avoid jokes that only work because of accidental variable names, UI hacks, or hard-coded formatting.

Later localization should support at least:

- German
- English

Additional languages can follow after the MVP.

### Content Escalation Rule

The first week should not be flat.

Every in-game day should add at least one meaningful content beat:

- a new guest behavior
- a new line type
- a new management option
- a new strange hint
- a new KASSANDRA behavior
- a visible shift in how the café feels

The game should remain cozy, but the player should feel that something is opening up.
